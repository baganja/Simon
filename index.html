My Journey to Gerontology

A Story of Love, Loss, and Purpose

In the remote villages of Tot, nestled within Kenya's Kerio Valley, my story begins—not with privilege or opportunity, but with profound love and an unshakeable determination to honor those who shaped me. This is the journey that led me to dedicate my life to caring for our most vulnerable elders, a path carved by heartbreak and illuminated by hope.

Growing up in Tot, a region shadowed by banditry and economic hardship, I learned early that life's greatest lessons often come wrapped in struggle. As the eldest of six children, I carried responsibilities that extended far beyond my years. My mother was a small business owner who ran our village's modest grocery store, her weathered hands counting coins late into the evening while ensuring our community had access to essential goods. My father served with the Kenya Wildlife Service, protecting our nation's precious wildlife heritage with the same dedication he showed our family—rising before dawn to patrol vast territories, his uniform a symbol of both pride and sacrifice.

"Public health wasn't just about statistics and policies—it was about understanding how communities thrive and ensuring every individual has the opportunity to live with dignity and wellness."

Despite their tireless efforts, our family's greatest treasures were my grandparents, who became my responsibility and my teachers. At Chebilil Primary School, I pursued education with a fierce determination that burned despite our circumstances. Each morning, I walked to school knowing that knowledge was our family's only path to a different future, but I also knew that my afternoons belonged to my grandparents. The challenges were immense—studying by lamplight after ensuring my grandfather had eaten, walking long distances while worrying about my grandmother's failing eyesight, and balancing academic pursuits with the tender care that aging requires.

My grandfather's illness became the defining chapter of my young life and the foundation of my calling. As his health declined with age-related diseases, our family faced an impossible situation. With my parents working tirelessly—mother managing the store that fed our family and father protecting Kenya's wildlife—I became his primary caregiver. It was a role I embraced with love but was painfully unprepared for. I was just a child trying to navigate the complex world of elder care with nothing but intuition and devotion.

We had no professional healthcare support in our remote village, no proper medical equipment, and no resources beyond our love and determination. I watched helplessly as my grandfather, once the strong patriarch who told us stories of our ancestors under the acacia trees, became bedridden on a makeshift mattress fashioned from old clothes stuffed into sacks. The floors that served as our beds offered no comfort for his increasingly frail body, and I spent countless nights adjusting his position, hoping to ease his discomfort.

The memory that haunts me most—and drives me still—is watching him develop pressure sores that began as small red marks but grew progressively worse. Without proper medical knowledge or equipment, I could only provide basic comfort while his condition deteriorated.

When my grandfather passed away, a piece of my heart died with him. But from that profound loss grew an unshakeable conviction that would shape every decision I made thereafter. No family should endure what we endured. No elder should suffer without proper care. No young person should feel the crushing weight of helplessness I felt watching their loved one slip away, knowing that knowledge and resources could have changed everything.

My grandmother, partially blind and struggling with the mounting challenges of aging, became my next responsibility. This time, however, I was determined that her story would be different. The pain of losing my grandfather had planted a seed—a deep understanding that specialized knowledge in elder care could transform lives and preserve dignity. I began to see connections between my academic studies and the practical needs of aging populations, understanding that my family's struggle was shared by countless others across Kenya and beyond.

This realization guided every aspect of my educational journey. At St. Francis Boys High School, I excelled academically while carrying the emotional and practical weight of my family's needs. Every lesson learned about biology, every essay written about community health, every grade earned was a step toward honoring my grandfather's memory and ensuring my grandmother's wellbeing. My teachers noticed my particular interest in health sciences, not knowing it was born from the most personal kind of necessity.

My path led me to Jaramogi Oginga Odinga University of Science and Technology, where I pursued a Bachelor of Science in Public Health with the intensity of someone who understood that education was not just personal advancement—it was a matter of life and dignity for those I loved most. Each course brought me closer to understanding the complexities of aging, the importance of preventive care, and the critical role of professional health services in rural communities like mine. Public health wasn't just about statistics and policies—it was about understanding how communities thrive and ensuring every individual has the opportunity to live with dignity and wellness.

The journey culminated in my pursuit of a Post Graduate Certificate in Gerontology Interprofessional Practice at Fanshawe College in Toronto. This wasn't just an academic achievement—it was the fulfillment of a promise made to my grandfather's memory and a commitment to my grandmother's future. Every lecture about age-related conditions, every practical session about proper care techniques, every case study about elder dignity connected directly to that young boy who had felt so helpless beside his grandfather's makeshift bed.

Today, as I work at Villa Colombo as a Recreational Aide, every elderly resident I serve carries the face of my grandfather. My formal studies in Thanatology—the academic discipline that examines death, dying, and bereavement—have given me a deeper understanding of what my family and I experienced during those final months. This knowledge has transformed my grief into purpose, providing me with the theoretical framework to complement the emotional wisdom I gained through loss. When I ensure their comfort, advocate for their needs, and provide compassionate care, I'm not just performing professional duties—I'm healing a wound that has shaped my entire existence. The pressure sore protocols I implement, the dignity-preserving care I provide, the family conversations I facilitate—each represents a victory over the helplessness I once felt. My Thanatology training has taught me that this work is not just about caring for the dying, but about honoring the full spectrum of human experience, helping families navigate their grief, and ensuring that every person's final chapter is written with dignity and compassion.RetryClaude can make mistakes. The specialized knowledge I've gained has indeed transformed my grandmother's life. She now receives the professional care that could have changed my grandfather's story, and when I visit home, I see her living with the respect and attention every elder deserves. Her partially blind eyes light up when I explain the latest developments in geriatric care, and she knows that her grandson has turned their family's greatest sorrow into a mission of hope for others.

This journey from Tot to Toronto, from helpless caregiver to professional advocate, represents more than personal growth—it embodies the transformation that occurs when love meets knowledge, when pain becomes purpose, and when the memory of one beloved grandfather becomes a beacon of hope for countless elderly individuals who deserve nothing less than dignity, compassion, and expert care in their golden years.

My Vision
